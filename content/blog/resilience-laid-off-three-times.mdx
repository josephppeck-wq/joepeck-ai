I've been laid off three times. Not fired — laid off. Company decisions. Restructurings. The kind where the HR conversation starts with "none of this is a reflection of your performance" and you spend the next few days deciding whether that's supposed to be comforting.

I want to write about this honestly, because the career narrative most executives present — a continuous upward line with no visible disruptions — isn't real and isn't useful to anyone trying to build something.

Here's what actually happened, what I learned, and why I think the disruptions made me significantly better at the work I do now.

## The First Time: CloudKitchens

Head of Sales, Central U.S. Four teams, fifteen markets, $20M+ in annual revenue. I was building something, and by most measures it was working — we drove a 300% increase in outbound demos in my time there.

The company restructured regionally. The decision wasn't about the team's performance. It was about how they wanted to organize going forward, and the role I was in didn't fit the new structure.

The weeks that followed were difficult in the way that all in-between periods are difficult — the disorientation of going from full-speed to stopped, the weird adjustment of not having 40 calendar items to attend to, the slightly too-long pause before answering when someone asks what you're working on.

What I learned: The work I was doing at CloudKitchens had taught me things about leading distributed teams through uncertainty that I couldn't have learned any other way. The 15-market complexity, the pace, the pressure of building in a company that was itself figuring out its model — all of that was irreplaceable experience. I left with more capability than I arrived with. That was mine to keep.

## The Second Time: Prokeep

Senior Director of Sales. Fourteen months that produced $1M+ in new ARR, represented 30% of the company's 2026 revenue, expanded pipeline 3×, broke into three new verticals. By any commercial measure, the role was working.

The exit was a strategic headcount decision. When companies optimize their cost structure, sometimes they decide to reduce senior roles that were built for a growth phase they're now pausing or restructuring around. The team's performance wasn't the question. The company's capital allocation decisions were.

This one was harder to metabolize because the results were strong and visible. There's a particular cognitive dissonance in being told that everything you built is valuable and then having the conversation end the way it ends.

What I learned: I had spent 14 months demonstrating that I could build in a constrained environment, execute across multiple verticals simultaneously, and produce meaningful outcomes without the resources that larger companies provide. That proof point — doing more with less — is actually more valuable in the fractional and advisory contexts I operate in now than a story about scaling with unlimited resources would be.

## The Third Time: Context Matters

I'll be less specific here, but the pattern held: company decision, not performance issue. Leadership transitions and strategic pivots affect organizational structures in ways that don't always correlate with what the affected person was producing.

Each time, the instinct is to look for the thing you could have done differently that would have changed the outcome. Sometimes that instinct finds something real and worth learning. More often, it finds confirmation bias in action — the human need to believe that outcomes are fully controllable when they're not.

## What Disruption Actually Teaches

The thing about getting laid off — especially when it happens more than once — is that it forces you to answer questions you don't have to answer when you're fully employed.

What do I actually want to be doing? Not "what job am I suited for" or "what pays well" — what work genuinely interests me, develops me, and creates value I'm proud of?

The answer I kept arriving at, across three transitions: I want to build things. AI systems. Revenue architectures. Teams that are better at the end than at the beginning. The consulting and fractional work I do now, and the tools I've published at [joepeck.ai](https://joepeck.ai), are the direct product of having time and space to actually pursue that.

The weeks between roles are hard. Anyone who tells you otherwise has either not experienced them or is managing a narrative. There's a specific quiet that descends when your calendar clears and you're not sure what comes next. I've learned to be honest about that rather than performing equanimity I don't feel.

## Why Disrupted Leaders Are the Most Dangerous

Here's my actual thesis: the best operators I know have all been through something. A failed company. A rough tenure. A business unit that didn't survive. A personal setback that required a rebuild.

Not because suffering is somehow purifying, but because disruption forces a clarity that comfortable success doesn't.

When you've had the rug pulled out, you know what the rug actually does. You make decisions differently — with more specificity about what matters and less attachment to the trappings of the role. You understand in your body, not just your head, that the organization is not the work. The work is the work.

The leaders I'd most want in a foxhole are the ones who've been forced to answer hard questions about who they actually are when the job title goes away.

## Choosing From Strength

I'm in a different chapter now — building fractional CRO engagements, advising on AI-powered go-to-market, publishing tools that demonstrate what's possible. The work is interesting. The building is genuine.

The difference between this chapter and earlier ones is that it's chosen. Not chosen in the sense that I planned it — I didn't. Chosen in the sense that the disruptions created the conditions that made it possible.

I'm not grateful for the hard parts. I'm realistic about what they produced.

If you're in the middle of one of those in-between periods right now, I don't have a clean bow to put on this. It's hard and the timeline is unclear and the social scripts for what you're supposed to say are inadequate. What I can tell you is that the leaders who come out of disruption building — with more specificity and less performance — tend to be the ones I most want to work with.
