I have sat through the MEDDPICC certification training. I have sat through the Challenger Sale workshop. I have read the Sandler materials. I have run the SPICED retrospectives.

I have also watched all four of these frameworks fail, at different companies, for different reasons, in ways that were completely predictable if you understood what each framework was actually designed for.

Sales methodology is not a religion. It's a tool. The right tool depends on the job, and most companies are either using the wrong one or treating their chosen methodology as an identity rather than an instrument.

Here's what I've actually learned from running all four.

## MEDDPICC: The Enterprise Framework That Rewards Patience

**My context:** I used MEDDPICC extensively at DocuSign, where I managed 70+ AEs and 6 RVPs with a $20M+ ARR quota. Enterprise software deals, long cycles, multiple stakeholders, significant contract values.

**Designed for:** Complex enterprise sales with 3+ decision-makers, deals over $100K ACV, sales cycles of 4–12 months. Situations where a single misstep in the political map can kill a deal that was technically a win.

**Works best when:** The rep has access to a genuine champion who can provide ongoing intelligence. The buyer has a real, quantified business problem they need to solve. The sale requires navigating formal procurement processes.

**Breaks down when:** The sale is fast. If you're closing $15K deals in 30 days, MEDDPICC becomes overhead that slows you down without proportional benefit. Qualification rigor should match deal complexity — you don't need eight qualification elements to close a deal that three calls can decide.

It also breaks down when managers don't reinforce it consistently. The most common MEDDPICC failure I've seen: reps learn the framework, check the boxes on discovery, and then drift back to intuition-based selling by month three because nobody enforced the framework in deal reviews.

**The honest take:** MEDDPICC's biggest weakness is that it's most focused on information gathering and least focused on what you do with the information. Champion development — which is the element most directly correlated with close rate — gets about 10% of the framework's emphasis in most training programs. That proportion should be inverted.

## SPICED: The SaaS Framework That's Actually About Retention

**My context:** I used SPICED at Prokeep as Senior Director of Sales. SaaS product, growth-stage company, mix of new logo and expansion revenue. The SPICED methodology's emphasis on customer success and long-term fit was specifically relevant to a recurring revenue model where retention matters as much as acquisition.

**Designed for:** SaaS companies with high customer success emphasis, where the initial sale is just the beginning of the revenue relationship. Particularly well-suited for products where expansion and retention drive the business model as much as new ARR.

**Works best when:** Your ICP and use case are well-defined. SPICED's Situation → Pain → Impact → Critical Event → Decision structure works beautifully when you're selling the same product to the same buyer profile repeatedly. It helps reps develop consistency in how they qualify impact and timeline.

**Breaks down when:** The sale is primarily political rather than value-driven. If the deal hinges on navigating complex stakeholder dynamics and procurement processes, SPICED's customer success orientation doesn't give you the structural tools that MEDDPICC provides.

**The honest take:** SPICED is underrated by enterprise sales leaders who view it as a lighter-weight methodology. The expansion and retention emphasis is valuable for any recurring revenue business — even ones running MEDDPICC for new logo acquisition could benefit from SPICED thinking for account management and renewal.

## Sandler: The Methodology for High-Rejection Environments

**My context:** I used Sandler during earlier-stage builds where the rejection rate was high and I needed reps who could maintain confidence and posture through a lot of "no" before finding the right conversations.

**Designed for:** Sales environments with high outbound volume, significant rejection, transactional or consultative cycles where the seller needs to qualify out aggressively. Also effective in environments where prospects are sophisticated and accustomed to being sold to.

**Works best when:** Your reps are experiencing call reluctance or confidence issues from high rejection rates. Sandler's "reversals" and pain-funnel questioning teach reps to maintain control of the conversation and not be defensive about their product.

**Breaks down when:** The sale is genuinely complex and requires collaborative problem-solving with the buyer. Sandler's emphasis on control and "not being sold to" can feel adversarial in deals where the buyer wants a consultative partner, not a tactical negotiator.

**The honest take:** Sandler is often underused for what it's actually great at and overused in contexts where it creates more friction than it resolves. It builds incredibly durable rep confidence and creates great habits around qualifying out early. Applied in a high-ticket complex sale, it can damage relationships that needed warmth.

## Challenger: The Methodology That Requires Great Reps

**My context:** I've observed Challenger implemented across multiple companies and ran partial elements of it at different points, particularly the insight-delivery and perspective-sharing elements.

**Designed for:** Commoditized markets where feature differentiation is limited. Situations where the buyer is sophisticated and has heard every feature pitch. Enterprise sales where buyers benefit from being taught something they didn't know before the conversation.

**Works best when:** You have truly distinctive insights about your buyer's business and industry that they can't get elsewhere. The insight delivery element of Challenger creates genuine differentiation — but only if the insight is actually insightful.

**Breaks down when:** Your reps don't have the research depth or business acumen to deliver credible insights. The most common Challenger failure: reps deliver a canned "insight" from a playbook that the buyer has already heard from your last three competitors. It feels presumptuous without the substance to back it up.

**The honest take:** Challenger is a high-ceiling, high-floor methodology. Applied by genuinely excellent reps with real domain knowledge, it's tremendously effective. Applied mediocrely, it alienates buyers who feel condescended to without getting anything of value. Most companies overestimate how well their reps can execute the insight-delivery element.

## The Hybrid I Use Now

My current framework borrows elements from all four, deployed at different stages of the sale:

- **MEDDPICC** structure for qualification discipline and political mapping on deals over $50K
- **Sandler** tonality and qualifying-out discipline in early prospecting conversations
- **SPICED** impact-and-timeline rigor for deals with significant customer success implications
- **Challenger** insight delivery for first conversations where I can genuinely teach the buyer something

The specific blend depends on the deal. Enterprise, multi-stakeholder deal in a commoditized market? Heavy MEDDPICC with Challenger elements in the first conversation. High-velocity transactional deal with an unsophisticated buyer? Sandler qualification with SPICED impact questions.

## The Red Flag: Companies That Switch Every 18 Months

The deadliest pattern I see in sales methodology is companies that implement a new framework, see 6 months of improvement as the new language gets adopted, plateau when implementation depth stalls, and then "upgrade" to the next framework — repeating the cycle.

Each implementation costs 6–12 months of organizational adjustment. The plateau isn't the framework failing. It's the company failing to enforce the framework at the management layer consistently enough for it to produce durable behavior change.

The answer to methodology plateau isn't a new methodology. It's better enforcement of the current one — which is exactly where AI changes the equation. Consistent, scalable coaching against any framework is now possible at a cost and quality level that wasn't achievable with human coaching alone.
