### Thursday (Tactical Framework: AI-Assisted Pipeline Review)

My pipeline reviews used to take 90 minutes.

Now they take 20.

Here's exactly how I changed it:

**Before the meeting (10 min):**
Feed your last week of deal notes into Claude with a simple prompt: "Analyze these deals against MEDDPICC. Flag where qualification is weak. Prioritize by risk."

**During the meeting (20 min):**
Skip status updates. Everyone reads the AI summary before they arrive.

Spend every minute on: What's the specific obstacle? What's the next action? Who owns it?

**After the meeting (5 min):**
Have the AI generate a follow-up summary with action items and owners. Into the CRM automatically.

What changes:
- Your pipeline reviews become strategic conversations, not status reports
- At-risk deals surface before they slip, not after
- Your managers coach instead of interrogate

The 90-minute review that burned everyone's Friday afternoon?

This is what AI integration actually looks like.

### Monday (Hot Take: Forecast as Leadership Problem)

Your forecast isn't wrong because your reps are lying.

It's wrong because you taught them that being wrong has consequences.

If "Commit" means your job is safe and "Best Case" means you're sandbagging, you've built a system that rewards fiction.

Reps don't submit accurate forecasts. They submit safe forecasts.

Here's what actually fixes it:

Stop asking "what will close?" Start asking "what is the deal doing?"

Days since last meaningful activity.
Has the economic buyer been in the conversation this month?
Is deal velocity accelerating or slowing?
Has the champion gone dark?

These are behavioral signals. They're in your CRM, your email, your calendar.

AI can synthesize them. It can score every deal against patterns from your historical wins — before your rep says a word.

The companies that are getting this right aren't getting better forecast calls from their reps.

They've stopped relying on rep calls entirely.

Forecast accuracy isn't a rep problem.

It's a leadership decision about what data you trust.

### Thursday (Framework: 3-Question Deal Qualification)

Before I invest one hour of management time in a deal, I ask three questions.

Not the full MEDDPICC stack. Just three.

**1. Is there a champion?**
Not a contact. A champion. Someone who believes in the solution, has organizational credibility, and is willing to spend political capital advocating for you.

If the answer is "we have a great relationship with the VP of Sales" — that's not a champion. That's a contact.

**2. Is the economic buyer identified and engaged?**
Not cc'd on an email. Engaged. Have they been in a conversation? Have they expressed a view on the problem?

If the rep hasn't met the person who controls the budget, it's not a deal. It's a conversation.

**3. Is the pain quantified?**
Not described. Quantified. "They're losing deals to competitors" is a description. "They're losing 20% of pipeline at the demo stage because reps aren't multi-threading" is a quantification.

You can only build a compelling ROI case around a number.

Three questions. Run them on every deal in your pipeline review.

Anything that fails #1 or #2 comes off the commit list immediately.

Everything becomes clearer.

### Thursday (Framework: First Outbound Motion from Zero)

Every time I've joined a company with no outbound motion, I've run the same four-step playbook.

**Step 1: Define a narrow ICP before hiring anyone.**
Not "mid-market B2B SaaS." Something like: "Series A–B SaaS companies, 50–200 employees, VP Sales or CRO hired in the last 6 months, selling to enterprise buyers." The narrower you go, the faster you learn.

**Step 2: Find 50 accounts that match — manually.**
Don't automate yet. Hand-pick the first 50. Understand why each one fits. This is how you develop pattern recognition you can't get from a tool.

**Step 3: Run 20 calls before you write a word of messaging.**
Don't write your pitch until you've had 20 conversations. The language your buyers use to describe their own problem is almost always better than the language you invent for them.

**Step 4: Hire your first rep after you have a repeatable motion.**
Not before. Never before. Hiring a rep to figure out the motion is the most expensive mistake a founder makes.

Today, I use AI to accelerate steps 1 and 2. But the logic is the same.

You can't automate pattern recognition you haven't developed yet.

### Tuesday (Adapted: MEDDPICC in the Age of AI)

MEDDPICC isn't going anywhere.

I've heard the takes that AI makes qualification frameworks obsolete. They're wrong.

What AI changes isn't the framework. It's the cost of executing it.

**Metrics:** To have a meaningful impact conversation, you need context — how the company is performing, what they've committed to, what pain is visible in their public statements. That research used to take hours. Now it takes 30 seconds.

**Economic Buyer:** AI can surface org charts, recent leadership changes, board composition. Who's actually controlling budget isn't always obvious. AI makes it findable.

**Decision Process:** AI can analyze patterns in similar deals to predict what evaluation process is likely. You can prepare before the prospect tells you.

**Competition:** AI can monitor competitor positioning in your territory in real time. You know what they're saying before it comes up in a deal.

The framework doesn't change. The speed and depth of information does.

The three weeks your rep used to spend developing a solid understanding of the decision process?

That information is now available in 20 minutes of AI-assisted research.

Use the three weeks on champion development.

That's the part AI still can't do.
